Data smoothing and density estimation

Dealing with "jittery" or "messy" data when we only care about broader trends

Motivation behind smoothing and density estimation

Put simply, we often just don't have time to perform the inordinate number of measurements required to get a "smooth" distribution if we were to plot all of our measurements on some convenient set of axes. Exactly how to handle this can become quite involved, however, and requires steps that may not be well defined by statistics (that is, we might just have to feel out some things). As always, it's important to start from a position of asking what the actual goal is and if we're willing to accept a result prior to knowing what it may be. For multivariate data sets, some of these methods have reasonable extensions while checking out kriging under spatial point processes may also prove worthwhile.

Histograms

While far from ideal, a histogram of data values can be an excellent place to start with any statistical analysis, even if it's not something that's intended for one's final product. Additionally, if one does want to attempt to use a histogram for some sort of statistical analysis (often not adviseable, but sometimes necessary or expedient) one would do well to simply "guess" a variety of bin widths for the histogram. While one might want to rely on statistically derived methods of choosing bin width in order to claim some sort of impartiality, there simply isn't much basis for statistical rigor in this field, though that doesn't mean nothing exists one might use. Namely Scott's bin width and Silverman's Rule of Thumb bin width.

Kernel density estimators

While histograms generally aren't good statistical tools (though they can be good for first order visualization), kernel density estimators can fill some of the void left for a more rigorous method of visualizing the distribution of data and smoothing over outliers. For R specifically, IQR() and density() are useful tools here, while the Epanechnikov kernel has served as a favorite "static" kernal in prior work due to it introducing fewer artifacts. While a variety of other non-adaptive kernels exist, adaptive kernels are their own useful field and tend to rely on local bandwith or nearest neighbor approaches.

Nonparametrics

While covered elsewhere in this guide, there is room here for a group of nonparametric smoothing methods that operate by searching for a function that minimizes some error (generally per some chi-square, least squares, etc.) when fitted to the data. Of these, the most noteworthy examples I've seen are the Nadaraya-Watson estimator, which I've gotten good results from, and various spline methods. While splines remain popular in a variety of fields and applications, I've personally found them somewhat prone to the same issues as histograms in that choosing a spline number becomes something of an arbitrary exercise where over-fitting data becomes increasingly likely and gets worse with more ill-behaved data. There do exist some methods for more rigorous application of splines and I've seen mention of a LOWESS method of fitting kernels, but overall I would recommend sticking to known kernels with known properties or the Nadaraya-Watson estimator.
